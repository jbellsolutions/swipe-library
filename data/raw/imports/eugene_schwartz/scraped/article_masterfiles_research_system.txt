THE EUGENE SCHWARTZ MASTERFILES - PART 1
How Eugene Schwartz Generated $150M in Sales Working 3-4 Hours Per Day

MODULE 1: The Research & Diagnostic System That Gave Him an 85% Hit Ratio

About This Course

Eugene Schwartz generated over $150 million in sales while working 3-4 hours per day. His hit ratio? 85%.

The course breaks down his process into three modules:

- Module 1: Mass Desire, Research & Channeling Demand -- The diagnostic foundation
- Module 2: The 5 Stages of Awareness -- How to match your message to market stage
- Module 3: Headlines, Mechanisms & Proof -- Writing headlines, mechanisms, and proof

Schwartz spent a full week on research for every project: 90% research, 10% writing.

Who Was Eugene Schwartz?

In 1949, a man walked into a New York advertising firm as a messenger boy. Five years later, he was president of his own million-dollar mail-order company.

Over his 35-year career, he generated more than $150 million in sales while never working more than 3-4 hours per day. In 1966, he wrote Breakthrough Advertising, which became known as the copywriting bible. Schwartz died in 1995.


The Rule: Don't Create Demand. Channel It.

The Story

In 1979, Eugene Schwartz wrote an ad for The Book of Internal Exercises by Dr. Stephen Chang.

The headline: "How Modern Chinese Medicine Helps Both Men and Women Burn Disease Out of Your Body Using Nothing More Than the Palm of Your Hand"

The ad was crude -- printed on cheap paper with small type. Colleagues called the envelope "ugly." Schwartz couldn't get anyone to run it, so he started his own company to mail it.

That ad ran for 14 years. It sold over $50 million worth of books. Dr. Chang made over $1 million in royalties.

Why did it work?

Because Schwartz didn't try to create demand for alternative health. That demand already existed. People were already frustrated with doctors, already looking for ways to heal themselves without surgery or drugs, but still wanting doctor credibility.

Schwartz saw that desire and thought: How do I channel it onto this book?

Schwartz's Metaphors

"Think of yourself as an atomic scientist. You find a tiny thing called the atom, which has enormous stored-up, locked-in power. And you find that if you take two atoms and bind them together, you can release the power. That's what you're doing in copywriting."

"You've got a market out there with a mass desire -- enormous, locked-in power. And you've got a product. Your job is to take that desire and channel it onto your product. That's it. You're not creating anything. You're releasing what's already there."

"If you think you're creating demand, you've doomed yourself to a lifetime of hard work and failure. Demand is too large for you to create. It already exists. Your job is to focus it onto your product."

"You're getting in a boat and letting the stream carry you. Just don't think you can paddle upstream."

Why It Works

Mass desire exists before you show up. It's in millions of people. It's huge and already moving.

Your product doesn't create that desire. Your copy doesn't create that desire. The desire is already there.

What your copy does is point that desire toward your product.

If the desire isn't there, no amount of brilliant copy will save you. You'll be paddling upstream and exhaust yourself.

But if the desire is there -- if the river is already flowing -- all you have to do is get in the boat.

Schwartz's first question was never "What should I say?" His first question was always: "What mass desire does this product satisfy?"

If he couldn't answer that question, he wouldn't take the project.

The Takeaway

"Before writing a single word, ask: 'What mass desire already exists that my product satisfies?'"

If the desire isn't there, no copy will save you.

But if the desire is there, your job is simple: Show them how your product satisfies that desire better than anything else.

Find the river. Get in the boat. Let the stream carry you. Don't paddle upstream.


The Diagnostic Process: The Schwartz Research & Work Framework

Here's how Eugene Schwartz worked. This is the complete system he used to achieve an 85% hit ratio and charge $54,000 for four hours of work.

Step 1: Choose the Mass Desire

What to do:
List 2-4 mass desires your product could tap into. Pick ONE.

Why it works:
"Every product appeals to multiple desires, but you can only use ONE in your headline." If you try to appeal to multiple desires in one ad, you dilute the power. The prospect gets confused. They move on.

Schwartz said this was the most important decision in the entire copywriting process. Most copywriters get this wrong. They pick the desire that sounds clever or interesting, instead of picking the desire that has the most mass power.

The wrong desire = invisible ad. The right desire = unstoppable ad.

How do you pick the right desire? You research. You read reviews. You listen to the market. You find the desire that shows up most often with the most emotional intensity.

That's your river.

Step 2: Extract the Vocabulary (Go Deeper Than Anyone Else)

What to do:
- Read the product (or interview the client) multiple times -- Schwartz did 4 reads for 600-page books
- Underline heavily on later reads
- Extract comprehensive notes organized by:
  - Benefits
  - Mechanisms (how it works)
  - Proof (numbers, testimonials, case studies)
  - Objections (what stops people from believing)
  - Emotional phrases (the language the market uses)
  - Aspirational identities (who they want to become)

How Schwartz did it:

Rodale Books was one of the largest health and wellness publishers in America. In the 1980s and 1990s, they generated over $300 million per year in revenue.

For every new book they published, Rodale ran what Schwartz called "the Super Bowl of copywriting." They'd hire two of the highest-paid copywriters in America. They'd send both writers the manuscript -- usually 600-700 pages. Each writer would create a direct mail package. Rodale would test both packages head-to-head. The winner became the control. The loser went home.

Schwartz competed in this Super Bowl four times in one year. He won three out of four.

His process? He read the book four times.

On the first read, he'd just read it cover to cover to understand the structure and big ideas. On the second read, he'd start underlining -- anything that sounded like a benefit, anything that could be a headline, anything dramatic, specific, or emotional. On the third read, he'd underline even more heavily, looking for mechanisms, proof, and objections. On the fourth read, he'd extract quotes -- word-for-word phrases from the author that could be used directly in the ad.

By the time he was done, he'd have 40-50 pages of typed notes. His secretary would type them up into what he called his "vocabulary file."

By the time Schwartz finished his four reads, he knew the book better than the editor who had worked on it for six months.

The Editor Story:

An editor at Rodale once came back to Schwartz and said, "This claim is too exaggerated. We can't say that."

Schwartz replied, "I combined something from page 116 with page 531."

The editor went back, checked both pages, and came back: "Yeah, okay! It can be done!"

Schwartz didn't make things up. He just knew the product so well that he could see connections the editor had missed.

Because he had those 40-50 pages of notes, writing the ad was easy. He'd go down the vocabulary file sentence by sentence, pulling out the most powerful claims, rearranging them, adding headlines where they jumped out at him.

The ad was 70% the author's words, rearranged. His hit ratio? 85%.

Meanwhile, other copywriters -- often better writers than Schwartz -- would read the manuscript once, maybe twice, and then start writing. They'd rely on their creativity, their talent, their flair. And they'd lose.

Schwartz's Explanation:

"The creativity is not in you. The creativity is in your market and your product. All you're doing is joining the two together. And the only way you can get the creativity out is to dig it out. And the only way you can dig it out is to dig more than anybody else."

Why it works:

You cannot write powerful copy without knowing the product better than its creator. Most copywriters lose because they don't have the facts. They rely on creativity. Creativity fails. Facts win.

Copywriting is not creative writing. It's diagnostic writing. You're not inventing claims. You're discovering them. They're already in the product. Your job is to find them, organize them, and present them in the order that moves the prospect from curiosity to desire to belief to purchase.

But you can't discover what you haven't looked for. Four reads give you the structure, the benefits, the mechanisms and proof, and the exact language. By the end, you don't just understand the product. You understand it better than the person who made it.

The takeaway:

Before you write a single word, extract the whole vocabulary from the product. Read it four times. Underline everything. Type up your notes. Organize them by category: benefits, mechanisms, proof, objections, emotional phrases, aspirational identities.

Your ad will be 70% their words, rearranged. You don't need great ideas if you can hear great ideas. And the product is talking. You just have to listen hard enough.

Step 3: Work in Focused Spurts + Take Mandatory Breaks

What to do:
- Get a physical timer (not your phone -- avoid the distraction)
- Set it for 30-45 minutes (Schwartz used 33:33, but any duration in this range works)
- Sit down with your vocabulary file
- Give yourself one rule: "I can do anything I want as long as it relates to the copy. I can't get up."
- When the timer rings, STOP immediately
- Take a mandatory 5-minute break (make coffee, shave, walk the dog -- anything but work)

How Schwartz did it:

Throughout his 35-year career, Eugene Schwartz worked every single day -- including weekends. But he never worked more than 3-4 hours per day. And he never experienced writer's block.

His secret? A simple kitchen timer.

Every morning around 10:30 or 11:00 AM, Schwartz would sit at his desk. He'd place a cup of coffee to his right -- always the same cup, always in the same spot. He'd swirl the sugar and cream the same way every time. He'd set out a pad and pencil in exactly the same space.

Then he'd pull up his vocabulary file -- those 40-50 pages of notes he'd extracted from the product.

And then he'd take out a small kitchen timer. The kind you'd use to time eggs. He'd punch in 33 minutes and 33 seconds. He'd press start.

From that moment, he'd give himself one rule: "I can do anything I want as long as it relates to the copy in front of me. I can ignore it. I don't have to touch it. I don't have to look at it. But I can't get up from the desk, and I can't do anything except ignore or relate to the piece of copy."

No willpower required. No pressure to write something brilliant. No goal except to sit in the chair for 33 minutes and 33 seconds.

For the first five minutes, he'd get bored. He'd look around. He'd sip his coffee. But because he couldn't leave, and because there was nothing else to do, he'd eventually pick up the vocabulary file and start reading. And within five minutes, he'd be working.

After 33 minutes and 33 seconds, the timer would ring. He'd stop immediately -- even if he was in the middle of a sentence. If he lost the thought, he lost it. That was fine.

Then came the mandatory 5-minute break.

During this break, he was not allowed to work. He had to do something else. Make a new cup of coffee. Shave (he was a terrible shaver and cut himself constantly, which meant he'd run back and forth between the bathroom and his desk). Play with his dog. Anything but work.

Why the break? Because creativity doesn't happen when you're grinding. It happens in the gaps.

Schwartz would later say: "My greatest inspirations come when I'm shaving."

This is the same method Mozart used. Mozart composed at a billiard table. He'd throw a white billiard ball against three cushions and focus on catching it with his left hand while his right hand held a pen over the score. While his conscious mind tracked the ball, his unconscious mind would slip him the next note.

Schwartz carried his timer everywhere. He called it "the most valuable thing I ever bought in my life."

Why it works:

You remove willpower. You trap yourself in the chair. Boredom forces you to engage with the copy. And because there's no pressure to write something brilliant, your unconscious mind relaxes and starts connecting ideas.

Your conscious mind can only hold about 7 memory bytes at a time. It's narrow, focused, logical. But creativity doesn't live in your conscious mind. Creativity happens in your unconscious mind, where millions of ideas, memories, images, and connections swirl together.

The problem? Your conscious mind blocks access to your unconscious mind when it's stressed, pressured, or goal-focused. The timer removes the pressure. You're not trying to write great copy. You're just sitting in a chair for 30-45 minutes. That's it.

The 5-minute break is where the magic happens. When you make coffee or shave, your conscious mind focuses on that trivial task. And while it's distracted, your unconscious mind connects ideas you didn't know were connected. Schwartz would leave the bathroom with headlines. He'd come back from making coffee with entire opening paragraphs.

The takeaway:

Never work for more than 30-45 minutes without a break. You're not being lazy. You're not wasting time. You're letting your unconscious mind do what it does best: connect ideas.

Inspiration doesn't come from grinding. It comes from the gaps between grinding.

Get a physical timer (not your phone). Set it for 30-45 minutes. Press start. Trap yourself in the chair. Let boredom do the work.

Step 4: Build the Ad as a Mosaic

What to do:
- Go down your 40-page vocabulary file sentence by sentence
- Pull out the most powerful claims
- Rearrange them into a logical sequence: problem -> solution -> mechanism -> proof -> offer
- Add headlines where they jump out at you
- Every sentence is a branch. Words are leaves. First the structure, then the beauty.

Why it works:

You're not creating. You're assembling. The client's words + your market insights = the ad.

Schwartz's approach:

"The ad is built as a mosaic. Every sentence is a branch on a tree. The words are the leaves on the branch. First the branch comes -- that's the bare outline of the sentence. And then you see the leaves popping up. The leaves give the branch color, beauty, strength, and power."

Start with structure. Add beauty later.


The Rules (Non-Negotiable)

1. You cannot create demand. It already exists. Find it. Channel it.

2. The creativity is in the product + market, not in you. Dig it out.

3. Work harder than everyone else = make more money than everyone else. "The will to win depends on the will to prepare."

4. If you don't spend 2 hours/week learning where your market is, your career will last 3 years. Stay in touch. Read junk. Watch box office hits. Talk to cab drivers.

5. Think in decimal points and percentage lifts, not in "good copy." Schwartz thought in 3% lifts, 10% lifts, 20% lifts. That's how he hit 85%. He measured everything.
