EUGENE M. SCHWARTZ (1927-1995)
Complete Biography

Born: March 18, 1927, in Butte, Montana
Died: 1995
Education: University of Washington

EARLY LIFE AND CAREER:

Eugene M. Schwartz was born in Butte, Montana, a small town of 30,000 people. He grew up there and left when he was 15. He studied at the University of Washington.

In 1949, he moved to New York City to work for the advertising firm of Huber Hoge & Sons as a messenger boy. He became a junior copywriter before the end of that year, a copy chief in 1951, and president of his own million-dollar mail order firm in 1954.

When he got out of the army he used his GI Bill to go to law school. He passed -- but, he says, hated law. When a friend suggested that instead he become an off-Broadway producer, he leapt at the opportunity. He produced two plays: "Time Of The Cuckoo" and "Clearing In The Woods". Both were acclaimed by the critics, and between them they won five coveted Obie Awards. Michael made $65 a week.

From there he moved to journalism, writing for large-circulation magazines, and then becoming a foreign correspondent. It was when he returned from Israel that he got into copywriting, first at Doubleday and then at Greystone.

COMPANIES AND VENTURES:

Schwartz operated several companies including "Instant Improvement, Inc." which published and sold health and self-improvement books through direct mail.

In 1949, his agency bought time on ABC television on a half-hour program. They didn't know how to fill it so they wrote a program a day. They created "The Answer Man" format and inadvertently invented the infomercial, selling piano courses through a demonstration-based TV format.

PROFESSIONAL ACCOMPLISHMENTS:

- Sold tens of millions of dollars worth of almost every conceivable product in mail order, both in his own firms and as one of the world's highest paid consultants
- Rodale Press once paid him a commission of $54,000 for four hours' work
- He took Martin Edelson (writer & publisher) from $3,500 to being worth over $50 million
- His "Hit Ratio" (the percent of winning ads) was 85%, one of the highest in all copywriting history
- He wrote one ad that sold 1.98 million copies of a single $25 book
- He wrote about 30 sales letters for Rodale Press that sold more than 8 million books and generated over $200 million
- His "Burn Disease" ad ran for 14+ years and sold over $50 million worth of books
- His space ads sold almost a half-million copies of his own book "How To Double Your Child's Grades In School"
- His "Flaming Torch" ad emptied the shelves of plant nurseries from coast to coast
- His ad copy moved a basement full of super-model Oleada Baker's books
- A campaign for a self-propelled fishing lure reportedly made $500,000 for his client
- His ads for Boardroom Reports helped sell 60,000 subscriptions at $36 a year

BOOKS WRITTEN:

1. "Breakthrough Advertising" (1966) - Considered "the most important book ever written on copy and creative" and a mail order classic. The "most stolen" book from public libraries because it is so rare and valuable. Copies sold for over $950 on eBay before republication.

2. "The Brilliance Breakthrough: How to Talk and Write So That People Will Never Forget You" (1994)

3. "How To Double Your Child's Grades In School" - Largely based on his vocational work with underprivileged school children in New York City's Harlem

4. "How To Get Thinner Once And For All" - Had sold only 7,500 copies in bookstores. After his mail-order campaign, sales rose to 150,000.

5. "Mail Order: How to Get Your Share of the Hidden Profits"

Plus approximately 6 other books (9 total).

WORKING METHODS:

- Worked 3-4 hours per day, every single day including weekends
- Used a kitchen timer set to 33 minutes and 33 seconds
- Never had writer's block
- Created 12 to 15 mailing pieces a year
- Read product manuscripts 4 times, creating 40-50 pages of notes
- Called his notes file the "vocabulary"
- Described his process: "Copy is not written. Copy is assembled."
- His copy was typically 70% the author's/client's own words, rearranged

PERSONAL LIFE:

Schwartz lived in Manhattan in a sumptuous apartment on Park Avenue with his wife Barbara, a successful interior designer. Their home was filled with their collection of modern American paintings and sculpture, and was in fact decorated to complement the art. They assembled one of the most famous collections of contemporary art.

Schwartz's working days fell into different parts. In the afternoons, he goes to the office and runs his corporations. In the mornings, he stays home, thinking up concepts and writing copy. Sometimes at night, even, he will leap out of bed, rush to his book-lined den and scribble down the ideas and themes which awoke him. He keeps a diary in which he records such themes; on the second day of the month, he already had filled out half a page.

He was a West-Coast person who never fully adapted to the East-Coast clock, getting up about ten every morning and starting work by 10:30 or 11:00.

ETHICS AND PHILOSOPHY:

Schwartz was a champion for the rights of advertising copywriters under the protection of the First Amendment. He argued that if an author made a claim in a book, then an advertising copywriter could also present this claim in the copy. He successfully litigated his own case against the government.

He says he won't do anything illegal. "Controversial, yes. Illegal, no." Then, he won't write ads for bad products -- or ads that in themselves are bad. He compared it with being an athlete who must stay in shape. "If you lose your honesty -- and if you break these rules you do -- you can't write good ads again, any more than an athlete who allows himself to get out of training can hope to win a gold medal."

It was not all that long ago that Schwartz was broke and his ethics put to the test: he was offered $7,000 to write promotional material for a land deal. He turned down the offer, he says, because he didn't believe in the proposal put forward by the man with the $7,000.

His father always said he had no money sense -- and died disappointed.
